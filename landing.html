The Neuroscience

Why good things disappear
from your memory

This is not a character flaw. It's biology. And once you understand it, everything changes.

🧠
The Negativity Bias
Your brain uses two-thirds of its threat-detection neurons scanning for bad news. Negative events are stored automatically and immediately. Positive moments need 12–20 seconds of conscious attention just to register. They almost never get those seconds.
Rick Hanson, PhD — UC Berkeley
🌱
Positive Neuroplasticity
Every time you consciously notice and record a good moment, you are holding it in awareness long enough for it to transfer to long-term memory. You are literally rewiring your brain to see more of what is already good.
Positive Neuroplasticity Training — Hanson
📈
The Upward Spiral
Positive emotions captured today increase the likelihood of positive emotions tomorrow. This compounds over time. A documented record of good days becomes the brain's reference point for what life looks like — and what it expects.
Broaden & Build Theory — Barbara Fredrickson, UNC

The Story Behind DOPAmine
I was on the treadmill at the gym when I heard something that changed how I see my own life.

A podcast. A concept I'd never heard framed that way before. The idea that if you capture small good moments throughout the day — not at the end when you've forgotten them, but in real time, as they happen — you can literally rewire how your brain remembers your own life.

I had spent years feeling like my life was harder than it actually was. Not because bad things didn't happen — they did. But because my brain was faithfully filing every difficult moment while quietly discarding every good one. I had a false sense of my own life, built from an incomplete archive.

I started journaling small good things in real time. A smile from a stranger. Finding a parking spot. My son's grade. Five green lights. After 9 months, something shifted. After hearing that podcast, I broke the exercise down even further — capturing moments the instant they happened instead of at the end of the day. The boost was even better.

I built DOPAmine because carrying a notebook everywhere is cumbersome. But everyone has a phone. And everyone deserves a tool that helps them see how good their life already is.
