Dan Sullivan's Great Yesterday:
Why the Past Builds the Future

Dan Sullivan has spent more than forty years working with entrepreneurs. The most productive ones were not the most future-focused — they were the ones who had learned to account for what they had already done.

By Dopa  ·  July 2026  ·  8 min read

MyDopa is not affiliated with or endorsed by Dan Sullivan or Strategic Coach.

I was on a treadmill when it happened.

The Strategic Coach podcast was playing. The episode was called "This One Small Change Makes Every Day a Win." Dan Sullivan was sharing an idea that had stopped him cold at a dinner conversation — not his own idea, he was clear about that. He had heard it from Lior Weinstein, an entrepreneur in the Strategic Coach community.

Weinstein had been at a safari conference. On the final night there was a bonfire, and parents were going around talking about how they saw their job as creating a greater future for their children. When it was Weinstein's turn, he said something different. He said he didn't know much about the future, and he didn't know what his children would do with it. So his job, as he saw it, was to make sure they had a great past.

Sullivan said that stopped him. He had been turning the idea over for a year and a half, and for the past two months had been applying it to his own daily life. He called it creating a great yesterday.

The treadmill kept running. I was not moving.

What the idea actually is

The way Sullivan described practicing it: he wakes up each morning and asks himself one question — what would make today a great yesterday when I look back at it tomorrow?

Not what do I want to achieve. Not what is on the list. What would make this specific day, when it is behind him, feel like something worth having lived?

He described writing things down as the day went on. Not accomplishments in any grand sense. Just moments of consciousness — took his morning pills, finished the chapter he had been avoiding, stayed calm when a flight was delayed three hours, then another hour, then another, then sat on the runway for an hour and a half, then waited two hours for bags that never came. He said in previous situations he would have gotten angry. Instead he read his Kindle. He said that was a test of the idea — and it passed.

By the end of a day like that, he had written down close to forty things. And he had, he said, a vivid memory of what he did. He was conscious of it. And that consciousness, he said, had its own reward — regardless of whether the activity was important or not.

He was two months in when he recorded the episode. December had been his most productive month ever. January his most productive January. He wasn't trying to be more productive. He was just present for each day he was creating.

Why it landed differently than everything else

I had been reading about personal development for two years. Sullivan's own Gap and the Gain framework — the idea that measuring yourself against a retreating ideal produces permanent dissatisfaction, while measuring backward against who you were produces genuine evidence of progress — was already something I understood intellectually.

The Gap and the Gain is a book Sullivan wrote with Benjamin Hardy. It has sold widely to exactly the audience that needs it most. The core argument is direct: the problem is not in the quantity or quality of your achievements. The problem is how you measure. When you measure yourself against your previous self, you are in the Gain. When you measure against the ideal, you are always in the Gap.

I believed that. I just didn't have a daily mechanism for living it.

What Lior Weinstein's idea — and Sullivan's two months of applying it — did was collapse the time horizon down to one day. Not a goal. Not a transformation. Not a journey. Just: make today worth looking back on. Then go to sleep. Then do it again.

That is simple enough to actually do.

What was still missing

Sullivan's program, Strategic Coach, is one of the most effective entrepreneurial coaching systems in the world. The Gap and the Gain concept has changed how thousands of high performers think about their progress. But the program costs tens of thousands of dollars a year and is designed around quarterly human connection and peer accountability — an experience no app should try to replicate.

The podcast episode was free. The idea was clear. And yet the next morning, anyone who listened to it still had no daily system for actually building the practice. No way for it to accumulate. No structure that would still be running in six months.

That is the gap I stepped off the treadmill knowing I needed to fill.

Not to replace what Sullivan built. To extend the same underlying idea into the daily moments that exist between the books, the seminars, the quarterly reviews, and the bonfire conversations in Africa.

Weinstein said his job was to give his children a great past. Sullivan said his job, each morning, was to create a great yesterday. MyDopa is the system that makes the great yesterday visible — not just felt in the moment, but recorded, accumulated, and retrievable as evidence of who you are becoming.

The one thing Sullivan said that stays with me

Near the end of the episode, Sullivan said something I have come back to many times.

He said it is not even about whether the activity is important. It is the consciousness of doing it that has its own reward.

That is the whole thing. Not the size of the win. The awareness of it. The fact that it happened, that you were there, that it was real.

The brain discards most of what happens to you. What you capture deliberately is what you get to keep. What you do not capture dissolves — regardless of how real it was in the moment.

The great yesterday does not build itself. You have to create it. And then you have to keep the record.
