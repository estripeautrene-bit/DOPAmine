Dan Sullivan's Great Yesterday:
Why the Past Builds the Future

Dan Sullivan has spent more than forty years working with entrepreneurs. The most productive ones were not the most future-focused — they were the ones who had learned to account for what they had already done.

By Dopa  ·  July 2026  ·  8 min read

MyDopa is not affiliated with or endorsed by Dan Sullivan or Strategic Coach.

Dan Sullivan has spent more than forty years working with entrepreneurs — some of the most driven, high-performing people in the world. And across thousands of coaching relationships, he noticed something that did not match the conventional story about what makes ambitious people successful.

The most productive people he worked with were not the ones who were most focused on the future. They were the ones who had learned to regularly, deliberately, and specifically account for what they had already done.

Out of that observation came one of the most quietly powerful ideas in the Strategic Coach curriculum: the concept of the Great Yesterday.

What the Great Yesterday actually is

The Great Yesterday is not a journaling practice or a gratitude ritual. Sullivan's framework is more specific than that, and the specificity is what makes it work.

The idea starts with a question Sullivan asks clients to answer at the end of each day: what did I actually do today that was progress? Not what do I want to do tomorrow. Not what did I fail to finish. What happened today that moved something forward — even slightly, even quietly, even in a way that will only be visible in retrospect?

The point is to create a concrete, specific account of the day before moving on from it. Most people move from day to day without doing this. The day ends, the next one begins, and the evidence of what was built dissolves into a general impression of busyness. The productive days and the stagnant days start to blur together. The brain, having no specific data to work with, reaches for the available heuristic: how do I feel about how things are going?

That feeling is almost never accurate. It is heavily influenced by the most recent difficulty, the most recent comparison, the most recent gap between where you are and where you want to be. It is not an assessment of your actual trajectory.

The Great Yesterday is an intervention on that heuristic. It replaces feeling with evidence.

Why Sullivan built this for entrepreneurs specifically

Sullivan's coaching work is focused on people who have chosen to build things — businesses, careers, creative projects — that do not come with regular external feedback. There is no annual performance review. No grade. No authority figure confirming that the direction is right and the work is producing results.

This creates a specific psychological challenge. Without external confirmation, the ambitious person relies increasingly on internal assessment. And that internal assessment, unchecked, tends to run against them. The gap between where they are and where they want to be is always visible. The progress toward closing that gap is often not.

Sullivan observed that the entrepreneurs who struggled most were not the ones facing the hardest external challenges. They were the ones who had lost the ability to see their own momentum. They were working hard, producing results, building something real — and experiencing it as stagnation because they had no system for registering what they were actually doing.

The Great Yesterday is the system. A daily, specific, factual account of what was done — not evaluated against an ideal, not compared to what someone else produced, not weighted against the size of the remaining gap. Just: what happened today that was real.

The measurement direction that changes everything

The deeper idea in Sullivan's framework is about the direction of measurement.

Most people measure themselves forward — against an ideal, a goal, a future state they are trying to reach. That measurement produces a permanent feeling of deficit. The ideal always remains ahead. The distance to it may be closing, but the brain registers the remaining gap, not the progress. You can objectively be getting closer and still feel further behind, because the measurement always points toward what is not yet there.

Sullivan calls this living in the gap. It is not a character flaw. It is the natural result of a measurement system that only has one axis — forward.

The Great Yesterday adds the second axis: backward. What was the baseline a week ago, a month ago, a year ago? What is the distance between then and now? That measurement produces something the forward measurement cannot: visible evidence of movement.

The person who can see their own movement — specifically, concretely, in retrievable detail — experiences their work differently from the person who cannot. Not because their circumstances are better. Because their measurement system is more complete.

What the research confirms

Sullivan's framework is practitioner-developed — built from decades of coaching observation, not from a laboratory. But it aligns closely with what neuroscience has independently found about how the brain processes progress.

Rick Hanson's work on positive neuroplasticity shows that the brain does not encode positive experiences automatically. They have to be held in conscious awareness with some specificity and duration before they move into long-term memory. The brain that is not deliberately capturing evidence of progress is running on a systematically incomplete record.

Carol Dweck's research on growth mindset adds a related layer: the ability to see incremental improvement — to register that you are better than you were, not just short of where you want to be — is one of the distinguishing features of people who sustain long-term effort and growth. The fixed mindset says: I either have it or I don't. The growth mindset says: I am further along than I was.

The Great Yesterday is the daily practice that makes the growth mindset concrete rather than aspirational. You do not just believe you are growing. You have evidence of it.

How to use it

Sullivan's version is simple enough that it does not require a specific tool or format. The requirement is specificity and regularity — not length.

At the end of each day, before moving into tomorrow, answer one question: what did I actually do today that was progress?

Write it down. Name it concretely. Not "worked on the project" but "finished the first draft of the section I have been avoiding for two weeks." Not "handled a difficult situation" but "stayed calm in the call with the client even when they pushed back hard, and we ended up finding a solution."

The concreteness is not about dramatizing ordinary work. It is about giving the brain material it can actually encode and retrieve — specific enough to be real, retrievable enough to compete with the vague negative impression the brain defaults to when asked how things are going.

Do this daily. Not because a single entry matters much. Because sixty daily entries produce a body of evidence that fundamentally changes what the brain has access to when it runs its assessment of your trajectory.

The practical effect over time

What changes after several weeks of this practice is not motivation. It is not inspiration. What changes is the answer the brain generates to the question: is this working?

Before the practice, the answer is derived from feeling — recent difficulty, visible gaps, comparisons to others. After enough consistent capture, the answer is derived from evidence. The record says: here is what was done. Here is how the baseline has moved. Here is what is different from three months ago.

That record cannot be argued with. It is not positive thinking. It is not a mindset trick. It is data — the specific, accumulated output of a life that is building something, registered in enough detail that the brain can actually see it.

This is what Dan Sullivan understood from watching thousands of people try to sustain ambitious effort over decades. The ones who made it were not the ones with the biggest vision. They were the ones who could see their own progress clearly enough to keep believing it was real.
