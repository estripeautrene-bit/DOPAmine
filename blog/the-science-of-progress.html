The Science of Progress: How MyDopa Actually Works With Your Brain

MyDopa July 11, 2026 4 min read

You had a good day, right?

Somewhere in the mix, something genuinely went right — you navigated something thorny without freaking out, somebody said something nice, you finished something you'd been dreading for a week. And by the time you're brushing your teeth that evening, a good chunk of it's already gone.

Vanished. Vanished the way your date's name disappears five seconds after they tell it to you at a party.

This isn't about a bad memory. This isn't evidence you don't value your own life. This is what your brain does, automatically, to positive experiences it doesn't make a conscious effort to retain.

The Way Bad Stuff Sticks

There's a term researchers use for this: negativity bias. It's an ancient survival feature — a brain that remembers the tiger outlasts a brain that only remembers the flower, because one of those memories keeps you alive through another winter. That same setting, running in a normal 21st-century work week, means the criticism from Tuesday's meeting can live on long after Wednesday's compliment has faded.

There's a flipside worth knowing: what happens when a good moment does get noticed and held onto? Psychologist Barbara Fredrickson's research found that positive emotions do more than feel good in the moment — noticed and retained, they broaden what you're capable of seeing and doing, and they build on themselves over time into real, lasting resources. The keyword is noticed. A good moment that flies under the radar never gets the chance to do any of that.

"Our brains are like Velcro for negative experiences, and Teflon for positive ones." — Rick Hanson

Psychologist Rick Hanson frames the same divide from a different angle: the brain reinforces whatever gets repeated attention, and a good experience barely noticed can leave almost no mark at all. Left unchecked, that setting quietly tilts an ordinary life toward the negative, one unretained good moment at a time.

Why Writing It Down at Night Never Quite Worked

This exact gap is what a popular technique tries to close: writing down three good things before bed. It's a real, well-researched practice, and it helps — but it runs into a specific problem. By the time you sit down to write at night, your brain has already spent hours filtering out most of the day's good moments. What gets written down is a secondhand retelling of a fragment, already softened by the same bias it was meant to counteract.

The practice was never the problem. The timing was. Waiting until the day is over to sift for its highlights means competing against hours of active filtering, working against you the entire time.

When Timing Is Everything

That's the exact gap MyDopa is built to close — not by asking for more effort, but by asking for the effort at the right moment, while the good moment is still there to be caught. A win logged the instant it happens skips the day's filtering entirely. You catch it fresh, fully intact.

Catching it is only half the mechanism. A single good moment, however well preserved, is still just one moment. Real confidence comes from an accumulation of evidence — a record of having come through before. Psychologist Albert Bandura's research on self-efficacy shows that people come to believe they can do something because they can see the proof that they already have, not because someone told them to feel confident.

"One moment, then two, then hundreds of captured moments — building into a personal record made of things that really happened."
The compounding effect of captured evidence

That's the second half of it. One moment, then two, then hundreds of captured, organized moments that build into a personal record — one you can actually check yourself against, because it's made of things that really happened.

What This Means

None of this asks for a new personality trait or an extra dose of willpower. It asks for one thing: catch the moment before the day erases it, and let those caught moments stack into something visible.
